Camvanho

Campyloderes macquariae Johnston, 1938 and C. vanhoeffeni
Zelinka, 1928

The two species, C. macquariaeand C. vanhoeffeniare morphologically identical, and should probablybe treated as one morphospecies.

Short description

cone without outer oral styles. Neck with only 14 placids (opposite to the more common 16 placids); placids alternate between narrow and broad ones.

Species with segment 1 consisting of a closed ring, and segments 2 to 1 1 of one tergal and two sternal plates. Acicular spines present in middorsal series on segments 1 to 9, (sometimes also 10 and 11 – the character is subject to adult dimorphism), andin lateral series on segments 1 to 9.
The lateroventral spines on segment 1 are conspicuously long ( exceeding the length of the following three segments), and located next to a much shorter pair of ventrolateral spines.
Cuspidate spines are notpresent.
MTS, LTSand LTAS present.
LTASalways considerably longer than both MTS and LTS.

Adult dimorphism: Adult dimorphism isnot fully understood, but appears to be expressed in presence or absence of middorsal spines on segments 10 and 11.

Key characters and similar species

The complete lack of outer oral styles, the fourteen placids alternating in width, and the long lateroventral spines next to a set of short ventrolateral spines on segment 1 are all characters that are unique for the species, which makes it easy to distinguish it from all other kinorhynchs. The species shows closest resemblance to the two species of its sister genus, Ryuguderes iejimaensisand R.
casarrubiosi, but none of these species hasspines on segment 1, and in addition, lateral spines in these species are missing on segment 1 to 3.

Distribution and habitat

has been recorded throughout the world, and represent either the first, truly cosmopolitan kinorhynch species, or should alternatively (and perhaps more likely), be broken down into several cryptic or semi-cryptic species. Besides its wide distributional range, it has also been found in a range of substrates, from more regular muddy habitats, tocoral sand, Corallina officinalis (calcareous alga), kelp and sea grasses.
