Short description

Segment 1 consists of a closed cuticular ring, with deep middorsal and midventral, wedge-shaped incisions. Closing of the head opening follows a lateralcontraction, rather than the more common radial closing. Middorsal and midventral placids elongate and narrow. Segments 2 to 11 composed of one tergal and two sternal plates.

Acicular spines present in middorsal series on segments 1 to 11, and in lateral series on segments 2 to 10. Well-developed cuspidate spines present in lateral series on segments 2, 5, 8 and 9. MTS, LTS and LTAS present; LTASlength vary from slightly longerto considerably shorter than LTS.

Key characters and similar species

S. ponticusby presence of cuspidate spines on segment 2, but absence on segment 4, and from S. armiger by absence ofcuspidate spines on segment 6. It is, however, very difficult to distinguishthe species from S. lusca, as the two species have similar acicular and cuspidate spine patterns. The easiest way to distinguish the two species might be in the relative lengths of their LTS and LTAS. In S. lusca, LTS are about 60% to 80% of the LTAS length, while LTS in S. pacificusare 80% and up to twice as long as the LTAS. Other, more subtle differences includethe primary spinoscalids, which have only a single, distal internal septa in S. lusca, opposite to eight septae in S. pacificus.

Distribution and habitat

The species was described from New Caledonia. The description also mentions the presence of the species off Redondo Beach, California. Subsequently, it was reported from the Clarion-Clipperton Fracture Zone in the Tropical East Pacific. It was also reported from Caribbean Panama, but it is more likely that this species was Semnoderes lusca.
