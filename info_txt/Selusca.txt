Short description

Segment 1 consists of a closed cuticular ring, with deep middorsal and midventral, wedge-shaped incisions. Closing of the head opening follows a lateralcontraction, rather than the more common radial closing. Middorsal and midventral placids elongate and narrow. Segments 2 to 11 composed of one tergal and two sternal plates.

Acicular spines present in middorsal series on segments 1 to 11, and in lateral series on segments 2 to 10. Well-developed cuspidate spines present in lateral series on segments 2, 5, 8 and 9.
MTS, LTS and LTAS present. LTS only about 2/3 of LTAS length.

Key characters and similar species

The species is distinguishedfrom S. ponticusby presence of cuspidate spines on segment 2, but absence on segment 4, and from S. armigerby absence ofcuspidate spines on segment 6. It is, however, very difficult to distinguish the species from S. pacificus, as the two species have similar acicular and cuspidate spine patterns. The easiest way to distinguish the two species mightbe in the relative lengths of their LTS and LTAS. In S. lusca, LTS are about 60% to 80% of the LTAS length, while LTS in S. pacificusare 80% and up to twice as long as the LTAS. Other, more subtle differences include the primary spinoscalids, which have only a single, distal internal septa in S. lusca, opposite to eight septae in S. pacificus.

Distribution and habitat

The species was described from thenorthern Gulf of Mexico, and has subsequently also been recorded at several localities in thesouthern Gulf of Mexico. A previous reporting of Semnoderes pacificusfrom Caribbean Panama is also more likely to be S. lusca.
