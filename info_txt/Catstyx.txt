Cateria styx Gerlach, 1956

Short description

elongate, slender and cylindrical species. The trunk cuticle is extremely thin and flexible, and tegumental plate junctions can be difficult to visualise.
Primary spinoscalids are very long, flexible and palp-like. Placids are completelyreduced, and not visible.
Segments 1 to 6 are composed of a broad tergal plate, and a narrow sternal plate; the sternal plate gets gradually narrower from segment 1 towards segments 6. Segments 7 to 11 arecomposed of single tergal plates with midventral articulations.
An eversibledorsal organ is present between segments 5 and 6. When retracted, this organ is difficult to see, but when the animal is exposed to stress, it gets everted, and pops out like an elongate, anteriorlypointing organ, from the intersegmental area between the two segments.

cicular spines present in middorsal series on segments 2 to 4, 6, and 8 to 10 ( males with crenulated spines on segment 10), midlaterallyon segment 11, and in lateral series on segments 6 to 10 (males with crenulated spines on segment 10).
Lateroventral tubes are present on segment 5. Cuspidate spines are not present. MTS, LTS and LTAS present; MTS are always much longer than LTS and LTAS.

Key characters and similar species

The two species of Cateria are most easily distinguished by the dorsal organ, present in Cateria styx only.
In addition, C. styxhas (in addition to LTS and LTAS) short midlateral spines on segment 11. Such spines are also lacking in C. gerlachi.

Distribution and habitat

Thespecies was described from Macaé, Brazil, and has subsequently been recorded from other Brazilian beaches, and has also been found near Valparaiso, Chile, andin Angola (the species identity of the latter is slightly uncertain though, and should be confirmed). The species is found in beaches withrelatively clean quartz sand, typically buried down to 50-70 cmin the sediment, between the low tide and mid tide levels.
